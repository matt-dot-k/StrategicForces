The impact of nuclear weapons on international relations has been nothing short of revolutionary. Not only have they reshaped how we think about warfare, their existence has also permanently altered how states make political and strategic calculations. The end of the Cold War and its associated fears of arms races and nuclear war may have taken some of the spotlight off nuclear weapons, but thirty years onward, nuclear weapons continue to remain foundational to international security. This application aims to provide a tool where users can explore and visualise different aspects of nuclear weapons, from the evolution of U.S. strategic force structure to arms control, and from warhead stockpiles to the facilities that design and produce them.

How to Use

Click on a tab which you wish to explore. There, you’ll find a menu on the right-hand side with different sorts of data options which you can toggle to view different types of charts and tables. Once you’ve generated a chart you like, you can click on a point on the chart to get some information about the relevant line/point on the chart. There’s also tables included where you can view the raw data if you wish. Don’t be afraid to try out different filters and settings! There’s a lot of data here for you to mess around with.

If something isn’t working as it should, or there’s a new feature you’d like to suggest adding, don’t hesitate to reach out and let me know. I’d be interested to hear any suggestions you might have.

Notes

As always, there are a few limitations which one should keep in mind when using this tool.

  • Warhead counts are for deployed weapons only, and do not include warheads kept in reserve.

  • Nonstrategic/tactical weapons are not included due to the ambiguity of “nonstrategic” as a weapons category.

  • Due to the lack of availability of data on specific warhead deployments, as well as inconsistencies in reporting on gravity bomb modifications, data on deployed gravity bombs only begins in 1998.

Suggested Resources

If you’re interested in learning more about nuclear weapons and have some time to spare, I’ve compiled a list of books below. Spanning a range of topics, like the quest to find a nuclear strategy, the development of nuclear weapons, as well as the policies and systems surrounding their employment, they’re a great place to start if you want to dive deeper into the topic. Check them out!

  • Bernard Brodie, Strategy in the Missile Age

  • Ashton B. Carter, John Steinbruner, and Charles A. Zraket (editors), Managing Nuclear Operations

  • Lawrence Freedman and Jeffrey Michaels, The Evolution of Nuclear Strategy, 4th Edition

  • Charles L. Glaser, Analyzing Strategic Nuclear Policy

  • Robert Jervis, The Illogic of American Nuclear Strategy

  • Herman Kahn, On Thermonuclear War

  • Matthew Kroenig, The Logic of American Nuclear Strategy

  • Keir Lieber and Daryl Press, The Myth of the Nuclear Revolution

  • Vipin Narang, Nuclear Strategy in the Modern Era

  • Richard Rhodes, Dark Sun: The Making of the Hydrogen Bomb

  • Thomas Schelling, Arms and Influence

  • Eric Schlosser, Command and Control: Nuclear Weapons, the Damascus Incident, and the Illusion of Safety

  • Alex Wellerstein, Restricted Data: The History of Nuclear Secrecy in the United States
